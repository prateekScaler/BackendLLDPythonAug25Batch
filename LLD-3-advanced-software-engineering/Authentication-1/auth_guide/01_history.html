History of Authentication

5,000 years of proving "I am who I say I am"

Why Study History?

Authentication isn't a modern invention. Humans have needed to prove identity since the dawn of civilization. Understanding this history helps us appreciate why modern solutions work the way they do.

Timeline: Authentication Through the Ages

3500 BCE - Mesopotamia

Cylinder Seals

Mesopotamian merchants rolled unique carved cylinders onto clay tablets to authenticate documents. Each seal was unique - like the world's first private key!

2000 BCE - Babylon

First Biometrics

Babylonian merchants pressed fingerprints into clay tablets to authenticate business transactions. Yes, biometrics are 4,000 years old!

200 BCE - Rome

The First Passwords

Roman military used "watchwords" (tessera) - wooden tablets with daily passwords passed through the ranks. Wrong password = potential execution as a spy.

1000 CE - Middle Ages

Signet Rings & Wax Seals

Kings and nobles used unique ring impressions in wax to authenticate letters. This provided non-repudiation - you couldn't deny sending a sealed letter.

1962 - MIT

First Computer Password (and First Hack!)

MIT's CTSS system introduced passwords. Within months, a PhD student printed the password file (stored in plaintext!) to steal computing time. The first password breach in history.

1976 - Unix

Password Hashing Invented

Robert Morris Sr. and Ken Thompson created Unix crypt() - the first password hashing function. They intentionally made it slow (1 second on 1976 hardware).

1999 - Present

BCrypt Era

BCrypt introduced adaptive cost factors - as computers got faster, the hashing could be made slower. Still the standard today.
